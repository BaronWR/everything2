Why change the Level Advancement System?

E2 is unique with respect to its wide diversity of topics that can be found: factual knowledge, poetry, politics, science, history... Name any subject and it is represented in some form or another. This content is added by a group of noders with a very diverse background, culture and motivation to do so.

With this diversity comes a broad range of noding styles. Some noders focus very much on quantity; quickly add as many nodes on a wide variety of topics. Others take the quality approach: add well-researched, in-depth writeups on a topic. Of course there is a huge group that falls somewhere in between, and then we have the very few brilliant minds who can crank out 5 or more masterpieces in a day. There are many approaches to climbing the E2 ladder, but as long as the writeups are for the best of the database, any approach is fine:

"Node any way you want, as long as it is good for E2"

To be more concrete: I don't judge the addition of plain, non-explicated lyrics (leaving copyright issues out of the debate), complete books from the public domain, or complete and maintained databases (notice the emphasis). The test of time will tell whether a contribution is worth its bytes.

Now let's look at how the contribution of noders is valued: each writeup is rewarded (or punished) with votes, the noder accumulates XP, and ultimately gains levels. Climbing the ranks of E2 is a game with a fixed set of rules called the Voting/Experience System. This game element is to a large extent what keeps E2 ticking: noders are enticed to add content and evaluate work of their peers through voting.

But... not all writeups are equal. The Level Advancement system is supposed to reward both quality of writeups (through the XP requirements for leveling-up) as well as quantity (through the number-of writeups requirements). And that's where the rules of the game appear to be a little outdated. As I have showed in my recent statistics on noder data (http://everything2.com/stats), the experienced noder reaches XP requirements for leveling up much faster than the writeups-requirements. This has the following effects on the content of the database:

  • "Bad" writeups are not removed or improved. Instead, they remain hidden in the sheer volume of writeups. There is no incentive to do anything about this, since it results in loss of a writeup, or loss of noding-time that can be used to generate more writeups. Either way, it doesn't help the noder to level-up. Unfortunately that's the way some noders appear to play this game. It is quite discouraging to hit Random Node and being offered yet another vague, unfunny or uninformative one-liner. The low reputation of these writeups is offers no incentive to fix things. The writeup stays, unless by chance an editor or a god notices it. With 40,000 writeups, that's a hard task.
  • Noding for Numbers. In words, it is highly discouraged but in practice, there is nothing to stop a noder from adding a whole string of hidden, sub-par writeups, simply to level-up.
E2 is continuously aiming to improve itself. "Raising the Bar", to quote dannye's brilliant writeup. And he is right (isn't he always?). With this in mind it is unfortunate that we are stuck with a Level Advancement system that ---in effect--- only rewards quantity, and not quality. Agreed, it is only a game, but the rules of the game are no longer appropriate with respect to the way we play it, or the way we are supposed to play it. With that in mind, and with the support from many noders (although dem bones and JayBonci deserve a special mention), I investigated some improvements over the current Level Advancement system.

Summary of Discussion

On Wednesday, October 3, I released the Everything Statistics document. The discussion on the proposed Level Advancement system generated a large volume of valuable comments, criticism and encouragement. The proposed system was based on two criterions that I used to characterize noder progress: Median Node Fu and Median Node-Fu Product. In hindsight, there were several serious issues that were not addressed properly, namely:
  • The median is a one-point parameter that is sensitive towards targeted downvoting
  • The median is restricted to integer values, and is thus not a very responsive parameter.
  • The proposed system punishes current noders; noders will drop levels
  • Noders will change the way they write; they no longer add writeups that don't "score" well, and they will remove writeups to improve their stats..
  • There is no incentive to vote or otherwise improve the database, since XP has no more function for level advancement.
  • Is node-reputation a valid criterion to evaluate node quality?
To accommodate these valid points, I stepped away from the initial proposal, and modified he system to a reward-only system; The Honor Roll. Compared to the initial proposal, this system has the following advantages:
  • Every noder will remain at his/her level. No one is "punished.
  • Noding styles are not altered. Any content counts towards level advancement, but high-reputation content will earn level advancement more quickly
  • XP is still required to level-up; noders are encouraged to vote or participate otherwise
There issues with properly calculating the "average" node reputation, making the system less prone to downvoting, were left open in the proposal, and I will fill in those gaps in this discussion. The point of "node quality" is of course a subjective issue, but looking at the list of noders who will benefit from the Honor Roll, I believe that this system offers a significant improvement over the current system.

The Interquartile Mean as measure of central tendency

The major problem with the median as measure of central tendency is that it is a rather coarse parameter for determining an average. This is not because the median by itself is inaccurate, but because of the discreteness of the population; node reputations can only assume integer values (..., 0, 1, 2, ....). Since the median is the middle (value) of the distribution, it is always an integer (for an odd number of writeups).

The other problem, the sensitivity of the median towards targeted downvoting is not a flaw of the median by itself, as it is meant to describe random occurrences/treatments on a population (i.e. every writeup has the same chance to be viewed/voted on). Of course, targeted downvoting is not a random phenomenon. On the other hand, this makes the median an unreliable parameter to obtain a noder's average reputation, and this issue must be addressed.

A more robust method to obtain an average node-reputation would incorporate more datapoints; for instance, we could use an average of the 1st quartile, median and 3rd quartile as estimates (such as proposed by 1010011010). However, this system is still prone to targeted downvoting around those three points.

To obtain more robustness, we need to incorporate even more datapoints. Yerricde's proposal to use the Interquartile Mean (IQM) proved to be the most promising. I also investigated the Interdecile Mean, but hopefully the following plot will make it clear why this measure (or even wider ranges, up to the geometric mean) are less appropriate as a measure of central tendency.

The following plot shows why the writeup-reputation distribution for ALL the writeups in the database, in cumulative form (e.g. approximately 50% of all the writeups has a reputation 4 or lower).

The black line indicates the range of the Interquartile Mean (it goes through the 25% and 75% values for writeup reputation). The red line indicates the range for the Interdecile Mean (10% and 90% values for writeup reputation). The area between the actual curve and the lines is a measure for the skewness of the range that we are sampling. In the case of a symmetric distribution, the IQM and IDM lines would go through the point of inflection, and in this case you would have a "positive" and a "negative" area, together equaling to zero. As can be seen , node reputation is not symmetrically distributed.

But more importantly, there are fewer datapoints at the extremes of the plot (i.e. low and high reputation). As a result, the accuracy of these values is less; there is greater confidence in the value of the quartiles than the deciles. Now visualize the area that is enclosed by the line for the interquartile mean and the reputation-curve. Slide the quartile points over a little, and the enclosed area will change its value slightly. Now do the same thing with the area enclosed by the decile points; a small change in the value of the deciles significantly changes size of the enclosed area. You can also draw a line going through the begin and endpoints, representing the geometric mean, and you will see the drastic effect of peak skewness.

In words, the inaccuracy of the endpoints significantly affects the amount of skewness that is incorporated in the average. As a result, the Interquartile Mean is a more appropriate measure of central tendency for this distribution. Since we are using exactly 50% of all the writeup-reputations, the Interquartile Mean is also relatively stable with respect to mass downvoting.

This high degree of symmetry further suggests that the IQM is a stable and unbiased measure of central tendency for the node reputation distributions that we encounter.     • Meet the regular XP requirements.
    • Obtained Level 2 through the Regular Requirements.

  • Level advancement in the Honor Roll goes according to IQMP; the product of the IQM and the number of writeups.
  • Noders in the Honor Roll with an IQM>=10 will advance as if they have an IQM=10 (A "cap" on IQM)
The requirements of the Honor Roll are set up as follows. The IQMP requirements in the Honor Roll were set to 5 times the number of writeups required, since 5 is the IQM entry level for the Honor Roll. As a result, a noder with an IQM=5 (exactly), will advance through the levels according to the Regular Requirements. Noders with a higher average node reputation (IQM) than 5 will require fewer writeups to level up. E.g., a noder at IQM=8 needs to meet only 5/8 = 62.5% of the Regular writeup requirements.

The cap on the IQM is set at twice IQM value for the entry level. This assures that a noder must node at least half of the writeups required for leveling up according to the Regular requirements (5/10 = 50%).

The XP requirements always apply for leveling up; e.g. a noder with IQM=7 and 500 writeups (IQMP=3500) must have at least 2900 XP to advance to Level 8. This rule encourages voting participation.

The entry level, and cap can easily be adjusted in the future. This may be necessary when vote-inflation becomes an issue (more noders will input more votes to the system), or the average quality of the writeups significantly improves (that's the ultimate goal of this system...)

Noders following the Regular requirements will NEVER drop any levels. Noders in the Honor Roll may level down when we raise the Honor Roll entry level in the future.

The Honor Roll is a reward for high quality noding that must be earned. Noders entering the Honor Roll should be aware that we will keep raising the bar.

Implications of the Honor Roll

For now, I have set the entry level of the Honor Roll at IQM=5 and a cap at IQM=10. These values could still be adjusted. Several noders will immediately encounter a level gain after implementation of the system. I have investigated the impact of setting the IQM entry level at IQM= 3, 4, 4.5, 5, and 6 (with a cap always 2x the entry level IQMP). Lower IQM entry levels are undesired, because the Honor Roll entry would fall below the average IQMP of all noders. Higher IQM entry levels are undesired, since the threshold would be too high for many noders.

It is important to set the IQM entry level not too high, because we want to encourage as many noders as possible to enter the Honor Roll, and improve their writeups.

The most important conclusion from this data comes from the bottom row. If we select an IQM=5 entry level, approximately 20% of all the noders with 25 writeups or more will fall into the Honor Roll system, and see a direct benefit, either by reduced number of writeups required to level-up or by direct leveling up. Alternatively, IQM entry levels 4 or 4.5 would be acceptable. For higher entry levels, very few noders would see benefits of the system. At IQM=3 too many noders would level up too quickly.

My recommendation is to set an IQM=5 entry level, and a cap at IQM=10.

Implementation


"Live" calculation of the Interquartile Mean is too computationally intensive, since it requires collecting of all the node reputation data for a certain noder. This cannot be done with adding one or two simple counters (as would be the case with the geometric mean), since the Interquartile range is partially based on the rank of the writeup reputations.

We can calculate IQM every night during database backup. The IQM will assume a fixed value for one day. This has the benefit that adding a new writeup does not show a direct drop in IQM (with possible loss of a Level).

The first step in calculating IQM is obtaining the data. JayBonci has timed this process, and it should not take more than 5 minutes. The second step is the actual calculation of IQM. I have devised a fast method to calculate IQM that only requires reading the reputation data once. A sorting routine would be much, much slower...

The algorithm places the node reputations in bins, from the lowest to the highest reputation. From this data, the IQM can be calculated very quickly. The method works since writeup reputations can only assume integer values, and thus the bin values are not range values, but the actual node reputations. The algorithm can be found here: IQMCalc.txt.

Disclaimer: I am NOT a programmer, so cut me some slack for not using a "real" programming language, and for the other quirky things that could possibly be improved in the code... DO let me know when you suspect there is a bug in my calculation method.

Applying the level-up criterion and calculating the IQMP from a fixed daily IQM value should be very simple.

I suggest a step-wise implementation of the Honor Roll System. For each step we should check what the effects are on the database, with respect to lag.

  1. Implement the code that retrieves node data during backup.
  2. Calculation of the IQM for all noders. This should first be done in the background, so that we can check the output before we release it onto the masses. The IQM calculation must be absolutely foul-proof.
  3. Implement IQM reporting on homenodes. Let noders get a "feel" for the numbers, and the effect of adding writeups on their IQM.
  4. In the background, calculate level gains for noders (without actually processing the level-ups).
  5. Implement the full Honor Roll system, with level-ups for noders that qualify
  6. Have a beer and be happy
