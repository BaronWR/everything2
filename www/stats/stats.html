E2 Statistics

Introduction


Perhaps you glanced at the Statistics nodelet and wondered about the sheer amount of nodes, writeups and users. But how come we never see the majority of these almost 40000 noders? Did most of these people actually write any nodes, or did they just sign up, never to return again... The content editors and gods are doing a terrific job removing nodes that don't withstand the test of time, but exactly how many of those poor writeups do we have around here?

Perhaps you visited Everything's Best Users list and gazed at the amazing number of writeups these people have accrued. Or how about those insanely high XPs? I could hear you say: sure that XP is not all from writing phenomenal writeups, so how do we all compare this? Let me jump ahead of myself: most of those noders on Everything's Best Users are there because they node, node and then node some more. And we're not just talking quantity... They're there because they node a lot, but first and foremost: they node well.

But how do we know? Somewhere cruising through the nodegel is the Average Joe Noder, and after some statistical analysis, we know all about him... We know how many nodes he makes, the votes he receives on his writeups, the XP gains, and how many times per week he changes his underwear.

Average Joe is everyone. On Saturday September 29, 2001 JayBonci gathered ALL the node data from the database and crunched it down into some usable form. The data was post-processed and analyzed by Professor Pi. For a better understanding and a motivation of some of the parameters that are reported here, please visit the writeups on (Median) Node-Fu and Median Node-Fu Product.

Some of the data was left out from this statistical analysis:

  • users with zero writeups
  • Webster 1913
  • some noders (less than 50) whose data import was broken somehow

Level Statistics

At the time of the backup, the database consisted of approximately 12400 noders with one or more writeups.An overwhelming majority of these noders (82.6%) is currently at Level 1. Beyond Level 1, the percentages drop drastically; 8.5% of the noders is Level 2, and 4.1% is in Level 3. Detailed statistics can be seen in the following bar chart.

Even though E2 has a large noderbase -- the 12400 contributing noders is probably a more useful statistic than the 39000 noders that are listed in the Statistics Nodelet -- only 17.4% of all noders with one or more writeups eventually make it to Level 2. In other words:

Everything2 retains approximately 17% of its userbase.

If we look at the total number of writeups that each Level contributes to the nodegel, we would perhaps expect a significant share of the lower level noders. Even though it requires a large number of nodes for (for instance) Level 10, there are only 25 of them (or 0.2% of all contributing noders). The following Pie-Chart shows the node-contribution per level:

The largest contribution to the nodebase is from Level 3, but the few high-level noders contribute large numbers to the nodegel as well. Note also the Lone Level 13'er (hmm, who might that be?), who is single-handedly responsible for 1.3% of the nodebase.

So how much XP does the average noder collect? Of course this is dependent on what Level the noder is at. We can calculate the average number of nodes for each level, and the average XP for the level. Since there are some noders with very high XP from voting, blessings or otherwise, and some noders with very low XP (the ones who Decline Voting XP), a better measure of central tendency is in this case the median. The following plot shows the average (median) XP as a function of the average number of writeups for the Levels 1-11.

The solid line with red markers indicates the XP/Level requirements for leveling up. From the plot, we can see that the average noder meets the XP requirements long before reaching the writeup requirement. In fact, line indicating the average XP diverges from the XP requirement line for higher numbers of writeups (Levels). As a result:

Higher Level Noders meet XP requirements before reaching the Writeup Requirement.

This statement seems obvious, as most noders of Level 3 and above will tell you, but it is an important conclusion. The graphical representation shows how much the XP requirements could be increased to make it a more it a more stringent criterion for leveling up. In fact, the XP requirements for leveling up could be increased to match the linear fit of the actual data:

XP required = 14 x (#Writeups Required)

This would lead to the following requirements for leveling up:
